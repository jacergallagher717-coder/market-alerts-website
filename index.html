Lesson 1: What is Market Impact?

Market impact refers to how news events, economic data, and corporate announcements affect asset prices. Understanding this helps traders anticipate price movements and position accordingly.

üí° Key Concept

Not all news is equal. The market impact depends on:

  • Surprise factor (actual vs. expected)
  • Relevance to current market narrative
  • Timing and liquidity conditions
  • Credibility of the source

üìä Example: Federal Reserve Rate Decision

High Impact Scenario

Fed cuts rates by 50bps when market expected 25bps ‚Üí Major market rally

Low Impact Scenario

Fed holds rates as expected ‚Üí Minimal price movement

Lesson 1: What Are Options?

Options are contracts that give you the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset at a specific price before a certain date.

üìû Call Option

Right to BUY stock at strike price

‚úì Profit when stock goes UP
‚úì Bullish strategy
‚úì Max loss = premium paid

üìâ Put Option

Right to SELL stock at strike price

‚úì Profit when stock goes DOWN
‚úì Bearish strategy
‚úì Max loss = premium paid

üîë Key Terms

Strike Price: The price at which you can buy/sell
Premium: Cost to buy the option
Expiration: Last day option is valid
In-the-Money (ITM): Option has intrinsic value
At-the-Money (ATM): Strike ‚âà stock price
Out-of-the-Money (OTM): No intrinsic value

üìö Example: Buying a Call Option

Stock Price (AAPL): $150
Buy Call Option: $155 strike, 30 days
Premium Paid: $3.00 per share ($300 total)
If AAPL ‚Üí $165: Profit = ($165 - $155 - $3) √ó 100 = $700 gain
If AAPL stays at $150: Option expires worthless = $300 loss

Lesson 3: Understanding The Greeks

The "Greeks" measure how option prices change based on different factors. Critical for timing your entries and exits.

Œî

Delta

How much option price changes per $1 move in stock

‚Ä¢ Range: 0 to 1 (calls) or 0 to -1 (puts)
‚Ä¢ ATM options ‚âà 0.50 delta
‚Ä¢ Example: 0.70 delta call gains $0.70 per $1 stock increase
Œò

Theta

Time decay - how much value lost per day

‚Ä¢ Always negative for option buyers
‚Ä¢ Accelerates in final 30 days before expiration
‚Ä¢ Example: -0.05 theta means option loses $5/day in value
V

Vega

Sensitivity to volatility changes

‚Ä¢ Higher before earnings/events
‚Ä¢ "IV crush" = drop after news
‚Ä¢ Example: Vega of 0.10 means +$10 per 1% volatility increase
Œì

Gamma

Rate of change of delta

‚Ä¢ Highest for ATM options
‚Ä¢ Increases near expiration
‚Ä¢ Important for quick moves

‚ö° News Trading Tip: IV Crush

Implied Volatility (IV) spikes before major news events like earnings, then crashes after the announcement - even if you're right on direction! This is called "IV crush."

Example: You buy calls before earnings. Stock jumps 5%, but your calls only gain 2% due to IV dropping from 80% to 40% post-earnings. Always factor in IV crush when trading options around news events.

Lesson 4: Timing Your Options Trades

üìÖ Choosing Expiration Dates

1
0-7 DTE (Days to Expiration): Ultra-short term for same-day news plays. High gamma, extreme theta decay. For experts only.
2
30-45 DTE: Sweet spot for earnings and news events. Balance of time value and cost.
3
60-90 DTE: For multi-event catalysts or giving your thesis time to play out.
4
LEAPS (1+ year): Long-term directional bets, lower theta decay but expensive.

‚úÖ Best Practices

  • ‚Ä¢ Buy options with 30-60 days until expiration for news events
  • ‚Ä¢ Enter positions 1-5 days before the event
  • ‚Ä¢ Set profit targets (20-50% gains)
  • ‚Ä¢ Close winners quickly - don't get greedy
  • ‚Ä¢ Check IV percentile before buying (avoid if >75%)

‚ùå Common Mistakes

  • ‚Ä¢ Buying options the day of earnings (IV crush)
  • ‚Ä¢ Holding through expiration week (theta burn)
  • ‚Ä¢ Buying deep OTM "lottery tickets"
  • ‚Ä¢ Not having an exit plan
  • ‚Ä¢ Ignoring bid-ask spreads on illiquid options

Lesson 1: Position Sizing & Stop Losses

‚ö†Ô∏è Golden Rule

Never risk more than 1-2% of your portfolio on a single news-driven trade.

üìê Position Sizing Formula

Position Size = (Account Size √ó Risk %) / (Entry Price - Stop Loss)
Example: $10,000 account, 1% risk ($100), stock at $50, stop at $48
Position = $100 / ($50 - $48) = 50 shares

üõ°Ô∏è Stop Loss Guidelines for News Trading

  • ‚Ä¢ Tight stops (1-2%): For momentum plays with high conviction
  • ‚Ä¢ Medium stops (3-5%): For swing trades based on fundamental shifts
  • ‚Ä¢ Wide stops (5-10%): For high-volatility events (earnings, Fed)

üí° Pro Tip: Time-Based Exits

News-driven moves often exhaust within 30-60 minutes. Consider exiting profitable trades even without hitting your price target if momentum fades.
